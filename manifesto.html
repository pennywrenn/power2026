A People's Manifesto for the 250th Year

PREAMBLE: THE ORIGIN STORY

This started in January 2024. Before the election. Before the noise reached its current pitch. A private musing that became a public question that became an urgent mandate.

The question was simple. Why do people keep asking what the Democrats should or can do? It's simple: put out Power 2026. The alliteration is inherent.

That was it. That was the whole idea in its seed form. Not a policy platform. Not a party strategy. A campaign. A civic PSA. A cultural reorientation. And the reason no one had done it wasn't that the idea was bad — it was that neither party could release it. Both parties are implicated in the conditions that make this document necessary. You can't ask the people responsible for the broken window to fix it. You need someone else.

So we're fixing it ourselves.

Power 2026 is not a Democratic project. It is not a Republican project. It is not an independent project in the lesser sense of that word. It is the American Project — the one the founders started, the one successive generations have been obligated to sustain and revise, and the one this generation is being called to recommit to at the midpoint of the first five hundred years of democratic self-governance in this country.

We are at the halfway mark. The first 250 years are done. The next 250 haven't been written yet. That's why 2026 is the appointed time.

This is the manifesto.

The document you are reading began in conversation — in the stream-of-consciousness musings, the morning reflections, the late-night phone call with the husband, the notes to self in the Reminders app and the voice memos and the ChatGPT threads that have been accumulating since at least January 2024, since the pre-election moment when the question crystallized into a statement. The source material for this document is not academic. It is not think-tank. It is not party-adjacent. It is American. It is personal. It is a citizen's accounting.

This document is also, explicitly, a response to Project 2025 — the Heritage Foundation's blueprint for reorganizing the federal government according to a specific ideological agenda. The existence of that document, and the Democratic Party's response to it (which was to shout its name repeatedly without offering a comparable alternative), made Power 2026 necessary. The antidote to a blueprint is not an alarm. The antidote to a blueprint is a better blueprint.

Consider this the better blueprint.

This document draws on years of civic thinking — on the accumulated reflections of a citizen who has spent the period from the global pandemic of 2020 through the constitutional inflection point of 2026 actively thinking through what this country is for, what it owes its citizens, and what its citizens owe each other and to it. The thinking spans employment law and civil rights, the protest movements of the summer of 2020, the ineffectiveness of movements that were co-opted before they could be sustained, the tech economy and its relationship to democracy, the electorate being undermined by partisan politics, the decision of a significant number of Americans to switch to independent registration not out of indifference but out of principled refusal to continue laundering their civic participation through either of two parties that have both failed them.

It also draws on a spiritual conviction that runs through every secular argument: that we are stewards, not owners, of this country. That the covenant is not just political but moral. That "write the vision and make it plain" is not just a strategic principle but a calling. That the revelation awaits an appointed time, and the appointed time is now.

2026 is the year. This is the document. You are the herald.

PART ONE: WHY THIS EXISTS

Neither Party Could Release This

Here's the uncomfortable truth that makes Power 2026 necessary: both parties created the conditions it responds to. You cannot be genuinely implicated in a system's failures and also be the credible author of the alternative. That's not cynicism — that's a structural fact.

The Democratic Party, for all its stated commitments to democracy, failed its own voters with one of the most consequential errors of the modern era: allowing the 2024 primary to function as anything other than a genuine people's choice. When Biden was put up for reelection and the media was reporting that he was reconsidering, and it was clear he was being encouraged to run — and then we were robbed of a real primary — that was the moment the mask came off. That's when the break happened for many people. That's when the party revealed that it understood itself as the steward of the election, not the servant of it. That's when the switch to independent became not a protest but a principle.

As Penny Wrenn has said directly: "I switched my vote to independent and I didn't vote in the 2024 election... but this is what I've been doing instead because I understand my civic right and responsibility and I know that it is not just limited to one election. I worked in the midterm election in 2022. I saw what non-partisan groups do and how they act in terms of really negotiating what they think of as objectivity, but they do it in a way that completely undercuts giving benefit to anyone who thinks differently — and that's what allowed the anti-woke agenda to really seed and take hold."

Refusal is a legitimate democratic act. Not voting is not passivity when it is paired with authorship — when you are building the alternative framework while others are simply reactively managing decline.

The Democratic Party's issue — the systemic communication problem that has allowed a manufactured counter-narrative to dominate — is that they are so used to reactive outrage. "That's terrible. That's not how things are done. You just don't understand." They experienced it in the 2024 election messaging around Project 2025: they kept dropping the phrase "Project 2025" so it trended, made it a cultural catchphrase via the BET Awards and SNL — but the communication was never refined or unpacked enough. You made the branding more famous than the content. You made people afraid of a phrase without teaching them what was actually in the document.

The Republican Party, for its part, has embraced a governing philosophy that treats the Constitution as a tool rather than a covenant. Executive orders designed to circumvent the very separation of powers that makes the document worth defending. The weaponization of the legal system against perceived enemies — compelling major law firms to provide pro bono services to the administration under threat of retaliatory financial penalties. The appointment of justices for ideological outcomes rather than constitutional fidelity. The strategic rollback of regulations, DEI policies, and institutional oversight mechanisms that represent fifty years of imperfect but real progress toward a more equitable republic.

And let's be clear about the legal ecosystem that Trump is disrupting: yes, he is disturbing it. But the legal system is not the morally principled entity that lawyers on cable television are defending as though it were. The law is about questioning moral order and seeking moral order — it's about assumptions, about what's wrong, about infusing the framework with updated understanding. The legal system is also, in practice, designed to protect employers from employees, companies from users, and power from accountability. The disruption of that system by one man for personal motives is not the same thing as reforming it for democratic purposes. Both can be true at the same time.

Both parties are implicated. No one gets off scot free.

The Project 2025 Mirror

Project 2025 exists. It was published by the Heritage Foundation. It is a blueprint — detailed, organized, long-game — for how a particular faction of the American right intends to reorganize the federal government around a specific power structure. It covers personnel, policy, regulatory philosophy, and the strategic use of the presidency's executive authority. It is, whatever its politics, a disciplined vision document. It was written down.

There was almost certainly a back-room conversation between Donald Trump and someone from his team and the Heritage Foundation — congratulating them on so expertly branding it, while creating the political distance of plausible deniability. The Heritage Foundation created a brilliant public-facing document that gave the administration both a roadmap and a getaway car.

The Democratic response was to make the branding more famous than the content. The communication was reactive. "Project 2025 is dangerous." That's terrible. What's the alternative?

There was no alternative document. There was no comparable long-range vision for America written from the people's perspective, in plain language, nonpartisan in its call to action, rooted in the founding documents and oriented toward the next 250 years.

Power 2026 is that document.

The structure is similar to Project 2025 in its ambition and scope. But the alignment is fundamentally different. Where Project 2025 imagines concentrating executive power in the hands of an ideologically aligned class, Power 2026 imagines distributing civic power across every American who is willing to show up. Where Project 2025 consolidates, Power 2026 distributes. Where Project 2025 is addressed to an incoming administration, Power 2026 is addressed to the American people.

The 500-Year Frame

We are 250 years in. The founders — who were, let us be honest, just people — didn't architect a system for eternity. They solved for what wasn't working in the country they'd left. They were workers, tillers of land, men who felt innate insecurity in their livelihoods and their geographical locations. They were seeking to forge a new nation, a birthplace for the self-protection and self-preservation they believed was necessary for enabling mankind's manifestation of its power to succeed.

They were not the founders in the way we have made them founders. We have only deemed them an elite esoteric few posthumously. It is a figurative identity that, by virtue of the lifespan of man, they did not get to literally enjoy. They saw themselves as the eaters, not the creators. They saw themselves as the stock of society, not the shareholders who wielded certain powers. Little did they know that in creating rules for their own enrichment, they inadvertently enriched the lives of generations far beyond the scope of their imagination.

The workers, the enslaved, the spouses, the women — were not the people whom they had in mind. They were leverage points in the system. And man hadn't sought such an experiment before, so man had not had enough time to deliver on the full impact and repercussions of how man perceived not only himself but those other than him.

As Penny has reflected: "The chaos that everyone feels — it's not true chaos. What it is is that there is no way for the way the document was written itself to continue to hold up this country because we know too much of the mind of man born in freedom. We are the only country of our kind — wherein that was the birthplace, the intention of this land — and we have now come to a generational foothold on securing, identifying, ensuring and sustaining that freedom... we will stop reacting to one man's conviction that freedom is as freedom does. We are free indeed, and because we are all free indeed, it is now time for us all to ensure collective freedom as much as possible."

The 500-year frame matters because short-term thinking is the primary pathology of American political life. One election at a time. One news cycle at a time. One outrage at a time. We have been trained by the architecture of our own media to think in four-year cycles, in two-year cycles, in twenty-four-hour cycles. We have been robbed of the long view.

The Chinese government — for all the legitimate critiques of its politics, for all the reasons no one is asking America to imitate its system — issues publicly documented Five-Year Plans. In March of each year, following the annual session of the National People's Congress, a renewed plan is released. It is a living document — updated annually, not to reset the vision but to sustain it through incremental, adaptive planning grounded in evidence and reality. The long-term trajectory is maintained while flexibility is preserved. The plan holds itself publicly accountable to stated goals.

That is a model worth learning from. Not the politics. The practice. The discipline of public, written, documented vision — committed to a timeline, released to the citizenry, updated against evidence of progress — is a discipline that the American civic imagination has never successfully demanded of its own institutions. Every four years or so, both parties release platforms that are immediately forgotten after the election. No one holds them accountable. No one can. There's no mechanism.

Power 2026 begins building that mechanism, from the bottom up, starting with the people.

We are at the 250-year mark. The next 250 begin now. The only question is whether we choose to enter the next chapter with intention and vision — or whether we allow it to be written for us by the people who have always been more organized about their intentions than we have been about ours.

The PSA Lineage

"Got Milk." Two words. A campaign. A cultural recalibration of how Americans thought about a food group. You didn't forget it. You still remember it.

"Only You Can Prevent Forest Fires." Smokey the Bear. A public service campaign that embedded ecological responsibility into American childhood for generations. It wasn't partisan. It was civic. It was culture.

"A Mind Is a Terrible Thing to Waste." The United Negro College Fund. Three decades of that phrase in the cultural atmosphere. What it did was embed in American consciousness the idea that the intellectual potential of Black children was a national resource — not a private concern, not a family problem, but a collective loss when it went unrealized.

These campaigns didn't ask for your party registration. They asked for your participation. They assumed you were capable of understanding. They treated you like a citizen, not a demographic. They wrote a vision and made it plain. They put it into the culture and let the culture run with it.

Each of these campaigns did something that Power 2026 is trying to do: it made a civic idea into a cultural one. It crossed the line from policy to PSA, from institutional to personal. And the alliteration helped. Got Milk — two words. Only You — two words. Power 2026 — two words.

The alliteration is inherent.

Power 2026 is the next entry in this lineage. It is a public service campaign for democracy itself — for the idea that your participation in American civic life matters, that it is not limited to one election every four years, and that the country needs you now, in 2026, at the midterm, at the moment when the first 250 years give way to the next 250, and when every congressional seat, every state legislature, and every school board member is up for grabs.

This is what a nonpartisan, multi-platform, public service campaign designed to reawaken civic consciousness looks like when it is activated by the people for the people, without party affiliation, without corporate backing, without ideological purity tests. It is aimed at every point of voter disillusionment. It says: we are halfway through the first five hundred years of this national experiment — and now is the moment to restore democratic integrity from the inside out.

PART TWO: THE VISION MODEL

Write the Vision

Then the LORD replied: "Write down the revelation and make it plain on tablets so that a herald may run with it. For the revelation awaits an appointed time; it speaks of the end and will not prove false. Though it linger, wait for it; it will certainly come and will not delay."

— Habakkuk 2:2-3 (NIV)

Write it down. Make it plain. So that a herald may run with it.

This is the original content strategy. Before editorial calendars, before media kits, before brand guidelines — there was this: the prophet being told that vision without documentation is vision without power. That the revelation cannot fulfill its purpose if it only lives in one person's head. That even the divine expects us to commit the vision to text, to make it readable, to make it transferable, to make it runnable.

This is why Power 2026 exists as a document. Not a speech. Not a press release. A document. Because documents persist. Documents can be revised. Documents can be shared. Documents can be audited, held up against reality, and recommitted to.

The founding documents understood this. The Declaration of Independence is a vision document. It declares what should be true, not what is currently true. It is a prescription, not a description. When Thomas Jefferson wrote "all men are created equal," that was not a description of the United States in 1776. It was a moral claim — a stated aspiration that the country would spend the next 250 years imperfectly, incompletely, but directionally trying to honor.

The Constitution is architecture — a living structure designed for amendment, for expansion, for the inclusion of people the original drafters hadn't fully imagined. It has been amended 27 times. The first ten amendments — the Bill of Rights — were ratified in 1791, just three years after the original Constitution, because the document as drafted was already insufficient for the full promise its authors were trying to make.

The 27th Amendment was proposed in 1789 and ratified in 1992 — two hundred and three years later. The Constitution exists in long time. It was built for the long view.

The Constitution is not a finite resource. It might be our most infinite inheritance.

The Habakkuk Standard: Accountability Built Into Vision

The Habakkuk text is worth dwelling on a moment longer. The prophet is not being told to write privately, to journal, to process his anxieties in a personal document for his own clarity. He is being told to write on tablets — the public-facing medium of his day. He is being told to make the vision plain — accessible, understandable, not encoded in specialist language that only the initiated can read. And he is being told that the vision is for a herald to run with — that the document's value is not in its existence but in its transmissibility.

This is the standard Power 2026 holds itself to. Write it down. Make it plain. Make it runnable. Not a graduate thesis. Not a policy white paper. Not a party platform written in the language of professional politics. A document that a citizen — any citizen, without a law degree or a political science background — can pick up and run with. That a teacher can read to a class. That a pastor can cite from a pulpit. That a neighbor can hand across a fence.

The Habakkuk standard is also, implicitly, an accountability standard. A written vision can be audited. It can be returned to. It can be measured against what actually happened. The vision that was not written can be denied, revised, conveniently forgotten. The vision that was written is on record.

Every elected official who has been given power in the name of the American people should be held to the Habakkuk standard: write down what you intend to do. Make it plain. So that those of us who will be governed by your decisions can run with the knowledge of what you promised and hold you to it.

The Five-Year Plan as Proof of Concept

China's Five-Year Plans are typically published in March, following approval during the annual session of the National People's Congress. The plans are released publicly. They are not secret governing documents circulated only among the politically connected. They are the stated agenda of the government, written down, made plain, sent into the world for all to see.

The point of a Five-Year Plan — in the best version of what a Five-Year Plan can be — is not the plan itself. It is the discipline of documentation. It is the public commitment to stated goals. It is the creation of a mechanism by which the governed can hold the governing accountable to the vision that was articulated in their name.

Philadelphia's city government produces a Five-Year Financial and Strategic Plan, updated annually. The problem — documented by analysts, by the Pennsylvania Intergovernmental Cooperation Authority, by the Pew Charitable Trusts — is that the accountability mechanism is weak. Goals that are not achieved are carried forward, modified, quietly dropped, or reframed in subsequent plans. The cycle prioritizes adaptability over completion, gradually muting long-term ambition in favor of short-term solvency. It risks being, as one analysis put it, planning theater — producing credible documents without enforceable delivery.

This is not a China-specific critique. This is a universal organizational failure. And Power 2026 takes it seriously: a vision document without accountability mechanisms is a manifesto without teeth.

Which is why Power 2026 is also a civic accountability framework. Not just "here is what should happen" but "here is how you hold your representatives accountable to what should happen." The vision and the mechanism. The dream and the delivery system.

We laud the model. We are building the American version of it. Not a government plan. A people's plan. A document that belongs to the citizenry, not to the administration.

The Founding Documents as Living Architecture

The Declaration of Independence did not describe an existing reality. It described a desired one. It declared the moral case for a nation that did not yet fully exist, in a country where a significant fraction of the population was enslaved, where women had no vote, where property requirements excluded most white men from the franchise.

And yet: the moral claim of that document drove American history forward. Not automatically. Not without violence and sacrifice and setback. But directionally. Every major expansion of civil rights, civil liberties, and civic inclusion in American history was made by appealing to the founding promise as a standard the country had not yet met. The movement for women's suffrage. The Civil Rights Movement. The disability rights movement. The marriage equality movement. All of them said: your own founding documents make this promise. We are here to collect.

The Constitution's design is the design of a living system in tension with itself. Three branches. Two houses. Federal and state. Enumerated and reserved. The founders understood that power, uncontested, consolidates — so they built in the contest. They built in the friction. The friction is not a bug. It is the feature.

What is being dismantled in the current moment is not the Constitution itself but the friction it was designed to produce. The checks and balances. The separation of powers. The independent judiciary. When the friction is removed — when one branch accumulates enough power to operate without the meaningful constraint of the others — the constitutional architecture fails not in letter but in spirit.

Tension is where freedom comes from. Liberation is not running wild. Liberation is running willfully within the constraints of existence that make collective life possible. Even Paul had a thorn in his side. Jesus suffered. Suffering is part of the human condition — and the acknowledgment of that is not an endorsement of suffering but a refusal to seek its elimination through the imposition of suffering on others.

The 10th Amendment: the powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people. Three words at the end: or to the people. This is the constitutional seed of everything this manifesto advocates. The people are not the residual category. The people are not what's left when the government is done. The people are the source.

We are the source. And it is time to act like it.

PART THREE: THE TEN DOMAINS

DOMAIN 1: LABOR & DIGNITY

The Historical Context

America was built by labor that was often uncompensated, often coerced, and almost always undervalued. Enslaved labor built the foundations of the American economy — the cotton economy, the tobacco economy, the infrastructure of the plantation system that fed the industrial revolution in both the American South and in Britain. Free labor — including immigrant labor, child labor, and the labor of women who had no legal standing to claim wages — built the rest.

The Civil Rights Movement was, at its core, a labor movement — a demand for the right to show up to work with dignity, to be paid equitably, to compete for opportunity on the basis of merit rather than race. The same energy that fueled Bloody Sunday also fueled the demand for fair employment law. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the EEOC it created were born from this movement — but they were born imperfectly.

There is a crucial distinction that gets erased in popular memory: the people who marched were not the same people who wrote the implementing regulations. The table where the Civil Rights Act was negotiated was increasingly crowded with actors who were not at the bridge in Selma — technocrats, lawyers, business leaders, lobbyists who had their own priorities and incentives. The table that once had movement voices at it gradually shifted: what was once two or three people from the movement plus a handful of sympathetic lawmakers became three movement voices plus fifty previously invisible lobbyists and stakeholders.

That is why the programs built in the wake of the Civil Rights Movement are now under systematic attack. Because the attack is not against the moral claim — you cannot win that argument — but against the institutional translation of the moral claim. You can defund an agency. You can rescind an executive order. You can rewrite a regulatory framework. You cannot unring the moral bell, but you can mute the institutional systems that were built to amplify it.

The Current Crisis

The rescinding of DEI policies in the federal government — programs spanning forty to fifty years — is being framed as cost-cutting or anti-favoritism. It is neither. It is the latest iteration of the oldest American argument: who counts as a full citizen of the economic republic?

The employment law system is simultaneously a system that exists to protect workers and a system that has been designed to protect employers. In-house lawyers at major corporations — whose entire job is to help companies do what they want to do, to get away with what they want to get away with — operate without any comparable counterforce on the side of workers. The average person cannot navigate the legal system without a lawyer. The average person signed a terms of service agreement they did not read because no human could reasonably read it. The average person who faces employment discrimination has fewer resources, less institutional support, and more reputational risk than the company they're trying to challenge.

There is no lawyer in the room who is the voice of the people. When companies meet with lawyers, and those are in-house lawyers, those lawyers work for employers. The entire purpose of in-house employment law counsel is to intimidate every day people. This is not a Democratic or Republican problem. This is a structural one that both parties have allowed to persist.

People-Positive Directions

1. Protect the right to organize. Union membership should be as easy to exercise as the right to vote. Any barrier to joining a union should meet the same legal scrutiny as a barrier to voting.

2. Require living wage indexing. The minimum wage should automatically adjust to the cost of living in each region, not require legislative action to keep pace with inflation while the minimum wage falls further and further behind in real terms.

3. Establish a Worker's Legal Defense Fund, publicly capitalized, that gives every worker access to legal representation equivalent to what employers maintain in-house. The information and power asymmetry in employment law is not neutral. It is a structural advantage for employers that has been built and maintained by policy choice.

4. Make non-compete agreements unenforceable for workers earning under a meaningful income threshold. Non-competes exist to protect companies, not workers. Their overuse has suppressed wages and geographic and professional mobility for a generation.

5. Close the gig economy accountability gap. The workers who drive for platforms, deliver for platforms, and create for platforms are workers. Their economic relationship with their employers should not be defined solely by the employers' terms of service.

6. Require pay transparency in all public company job postings. The information asymmetry between employers and candidates is a structural disadvantage for workers that can be remedied by law without significant cost to employers.

7. Support the dignity of caregiving. Paid family leave, child care support, and elder care recognition are labor issues, not "women's issues." The economy depends on care work. Care work deserves economic recognition and compensation commensurate with its actual value.

DOMAIN 2: AMERICA AT 250 — THE REDEDICATION

The Historical Context

The Centennial Exposition in Philadelphia in 1876 arrived while the country was still in the bloody convulsions of Reconstruction and the betrayal of Black citizenship — and yet it generated a statement of industrial ambition and civic aspiration that was felt nationally. The Bicentennial in 1976 happened in the shadow of Vietnam, Watergate, and economic stagflation, and yet somehow generated a genuine outpouring of patriotic feeling, a shared moment of national recommitment.

The 250th is different from both of those moments because it arrives at a moment of profound constitutional stress — when the basic mechanisms of self-governance are under active contestation, when the independence of the judiciary is a subject of daily news coverage, when the concept of a peaceful transfer of power has been contested by one of the two major parties.

Which means it is also an extraordinary opportunity. Moments of stress are moments of legibility. When the system is straining, you can see which parts are load-bearing. When the values are under attack, you can see which values are worth defending. When the contract is being broken, you can see what the contract actually was.

The Current Crisis

America 250 — the organization charged with coordinating the semiquincentennial — has had leadership turbulence and limited grassroots activation. The 250th anniversary of the country is approaching without a national civic narrative, without a people-led vision for what we want to recommit to, and without a cultural consensus about what this country is for.

The keyword "America 250" and "America500" have been undertracked in public discourse. The commercial and governmental apparatus around the anniversary is being built without the civic soul that would make it meaningful. We are letting the anniversary of our founding pass without the founding — without the act of articulation that was the founding's most essential gesture.

We need a new act of articulation. Not a government-issued one. A people's one.

The 50th president is two elections away. It is not too early to be thinking about the long game — about what we want the 50th presidency to represent, what we want to have already done by the time that 50th president takes office, what constitutional and civic infrastructure we want to have built. As Penny has reflected: "Wouldn't that be interesting — next year, 2026, when we're celebrating our 250th anniversary, to have goals that begin with the 50th president of the United States... How do we elect the council of founders? The people who we send to the constitutional convention, ideally by whatever year would be before the 50th president of the United States... thinking about goals that could make up a blueprint with different milestones that can be crowd-sourced, that can be activated along the way, that aren't so much about making noise as they are about establishing framework."

People-Positive Directions

1. Make the 250th a civic rededication, not a celebration. Not just fireworks and flags — though those too — but a national conversation about what the founding documents promise, how far we've come, and what the next 250 years should be oriented toward.

2. Convene deliberative town halls in every congressional district in 2025 and 2026 dedicated to the questions: What do we want to recommit to? What have we learned in 250 years? What needs to be revised?

3. Commission a Declaration of Freedom — a new moral founding document, authored by a broad cross-section of Americans, that names the values and obligations we are choosing to carry forward into the next 250 years. Not a constitutional amendment. A moral document. A companion to the Declaration.

4. Invest in civic education as a national infrastructure priority, on par with physical infrastructure. The country cannot sustain a democracy whose citizens don't know how it works.

5. Restore and elevate the constitutional framing of public discourse. Every major piece of legislation, every major executive action, should be preceded by a public constitutional accounting.

6. Create a 250th-year civic archive — a living document of the American people's testimony about this country, crowdsourced, searchable, publicly owned, and held in trust for the next 250 years.

7. Mark the 250th not with nostalgia but with intention. We celebrate not what we were but what we are choosing to become.

DOMAIN 3: ELECTORAL REALITY & VOTING INFRASTRUCTURE

The Historical Context

The right to vote has been won, lost, compromised, suppressed, restored, and suppressed again throughout American history. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 — born directly from the blood on the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama — was the most powerful voting rights legislation in American history. It was gutted by the Supreme Court's Shelby County v. Holder decision in 2013, which struck down the preclearance formula that required states with a history of voter suppression to get federal approval before changing voting laws.

Within hours of that decision, states that had been subject to preclearance began implementing voter ID laws, closing polling places, and purging voter rolls. This was not coincidence. The infrastructure of suppression was ready and waiting.

The Current Crisis

The midterm of 2026 is the most consequential midterm in a generation. Four hundred-plus seats in Congress, alongside state legislative races, ballot initiatives, and the races at every level of government that determine how power is actually exercised in American daily life. And the machinery of that election is under simultaneous attack from multiple directions.

Maximum partisan warfare — redistricting engineered for party advantage rather than voter representation — is the current system. Texas pursuing five new Republican seats. California debating whether to respond in kind through Gavin Newsom's intervention with the independent commission. The independent commission that California created, that has not faced a single successful legal challenge to its maps, that operates with transparency and without the thumb of the legislature on the scale — and yet one party is considering circumventing it for strategic advantage.

That is the moment we are in. The system designed to protect fair representation is being weighed against the partisan interest in winning. And the voters — who are the people this entire infrastructure supposedly serves — are the terrain of the conflict, not its architects.

Independent voters are the fastest-growing bloc in American politics precisely because people understand, viscerally, that neither party adequately represents them. And yet independent voters are systematically excluded from the primary process in most states. The primary is where the actual narrowing happens — where a field of potential general election candidates becomes two, one of whom will govern. Excluding thirty percent or more of the electorate from that round of voting is not a neutral act. It is a structural guarantee of extremity.

The civil war that is already upon us is not between Republicans and Democrats as citizens. It is between the parties as institutions and the voters they claim to represent. As the analysis goes: both parties keep the people against each other, which is why it's incumbent to rally as independents — to ensure independents can vote in midterms and primaries. The right to vote in primaries as an independent is a frontline issue because primaries are where power is first seized.

People-Positive Directions

1. Open the primaries. Every voter should be able to vote in any primary, regardless of party registration. The primary is a public election, funded by public money, for a public office.

2. Expand independent redistricting commissions nationally. California and Michigan and Arizona have demonstrated that independent commissions can draw fair maps that withstand legal scrutiny. States should not be allowed to draw their own congressional maps in ways that guarantee partisan outcomes.

3. Establish ranked-choice voting at the federal level for primary elections. This allows voters to express nuanced preferences and reduces the incentive for extreme candidates to prevail in multi-candidate fields.

4. Guarantee ballot access for independent and third-party candidates. The barriers to appearing on a general election ballot as an independent should not be higher than the barriers to appearing as a major-party candidate.

5. Protect early voting, mail voting, and same-day registration nationally. Access to voting should not depend on which state you live in.

6. Create a national voter information infrastructure — a nonpartisan public resource that ensures every voter knows their rights, their polling place, their deadlines, and their options.

7. Fund local elections offices adequately. The machinery of democracy is chronically underfunded. Election workers are chronically underpaid and increasingly subject to threats and harassment.

8. Reform the Electoral College through national popular vote compact legislation — an approach that does not require a constitutional amendment but can achieve the same effect when enough states have adopted it.

9. Invest in nonpartisan civic tech — tools that help voters track their representatives' votes, funding sources, and stated positions against their actual records. The information exists. The aggregation and accessibility do not.

On the Independent Voter Question

There is a specific argument worth making here, because it touches on a democratic paradox that has not been adequately addressed by either party, because neither party benefits from addressing it.

Independents are the fastest-growing voter bloc in the country. Millions of Americans have concluded — not out of apathy but out of principled refusal — that neither major party represents them adequately. They have looked at the parties, looked at their records, looked at the gap between what the parties say and what they do, and have declined the invitation.

These voters are then systematically excluded from the elections that matter most: the primaries. In most states, closed primaries require party registration to participate. The primary is where a field of potential candidates is narrowed to the one who will represent the party in the general election. In most congressional districts, due to partisan gerrymandering, the general election is already decided by party registration — which means the primary is actually the election. And independents don't get to vote in it.

This is not incidental. It is structural. It is the mechanism by which both parties protect their power from the very voters they claim to serve. It ensures that the people who make it to the general election ballot are the most partisan representatives of the most partisan primary voters — leaving everyone else to choose between two options, neither of which they selected.

The independent voter organizing priority for 2026 is this: change the laws. Open the primaries. Where that is not immediately possible, change your registration for the purpose of the primary — vote, then change it back. Use the system against itself until the system can be changed.

DOMAIN 4: MEDIA, INFORMATION & THE INFLUENCE MATRIX

The Historical Context

The First Amendment's protection of the press was not a gift to media companies. It was a protection for the public's right to information — the founding generation's understanding that democracy cannot function without a citizenry that has access to accurate, independent information about what its government is doing.

For most of the twentieth century, that protection had an institutional home: newspaper editorial boards operating under professional standards, broadcast news with fairness doctrine obligations, magazine fact-checking departments. These institutions were imperfect. They were captured by economic interests. They were not representative of all Americans. But they operated within a framework of professional accountability that constrained at least some of the worst distortions.

The Powell Memo of 1971 — Lewis Powell's confidential memorandum to the U.S. Chamber of Commerce — outlined a strategy for business to recapture influence in politics, academia, and media that it had lost to the regulatory and labor movements of the previous decades. It called for investment in think tanks, in academic chairs, in litigation, and in media infrastructure. It is the original blueprint for what became the Influence Matrix. It worked.

The Current Crisis

What we have now is not a media crisis. It is an information architecture crisis. The platforms that host most American public discourse are not media companies in any meaningful sense. They have no editorial standards. They have no call to action. They have no accountability to their users as citizens. They are engagement maximizers. And engagement and truth are not the same thing. Engagement and civic health are often in direct opposition.

There is a problem of reporters — even experienced, credentialed journalists — who operate without a computational framework for what they should be doing. How should a reporter speak? How should they interact with the user? What defines the press? What's the mechanism? What's the call to action? How is the reporter a representative of the press — what defines that representation, what's the standard, what's the civic function?

What constitutes "the media" right now is really just a content universe. Anecdotal journalism with a little bit of research and a viral hook. Front-facing user-interface personalities of news who are doing what they do without any agreed framework for what responsible civic journalism looks like in the digital age.

The Influence Matrix is the name for the full system: the campaign finance networks, the dark money structures, the lobbyist pipelines, the revolving door between government and industry, the social media algorithms that reward extremity, the media ecosystems that profit from outrage. It is not a conspiracy. It is a structure. And the people are inside it without a map.

People-Positive Directions

1. Treat social media platforms as publishers with corresponding legal accountability for the content they algorithmically amplify. Section 230 was designed for a different internet and needs to be revised.

2. Mandate algorithmic transparency. Every major platform should be required to disclose how its recommendation algorithm works, who it amplifies, and what the measured effects on user behavior are.

3. Establish a Public Media Investment Fund — national investment in local journalism, especially in news deserts where communities have lost their local papers.

4. Require disclosure of all political advertising funding, including digital advertising, social media advertising, and influencer-based political content. Dark money in digital advertising is an accountability void.

5. Create media literacy infrastructure in schools, libraries, and community centers that teaches citizens how to evaluate sources, trace funding, identify bias, and distinguish reporting from opinion.

6. Support the development of nonpartisan civic media that treats voters as citizens, not demographics. The PSA model — public service, civic purpose, nonpartisan reach — should be expanded and publicly funded.

7. Develop an annual Influence Matrix audit — a publicly available accounting of who is funding which civic narratives, which advocacy organizations, and which electoral campaigns, and what their documented interests are.

8. Protect investigative journalism through public and philanthropic funding structures that insulate it from commercial pressure and political retaliation.

DOMAIN 5: EDUCATION & CIVIC IMAGINATION

The Historical Context

"A mind is a terrible thing to waste." The United Negro College Fund created one of the most durable civic phrases in American cultural history. What it did was embed in American consciousness the idea that the intellectual potential of Black children was a national resource. Not a private concern. Not a family problem. A collective loss when it went unrealized.

That is what civic education is for. Not to produce compliant citizens who can recite the three branches of government. But to produce citizens with civic imagination — people who can see themselves in the system, who understand how it works, who believe their participation matters, and who have the tools to act on that belief.

Every moment of technological disruption in history has been accompanied by a corresponding need for education to bridge the gap. The Industrial Revolution created the demand for polytechnic schools and technical literacy. The early computing era required universities to build computer science programs. The internet era required a generation of self-taught digital natives to build the platforms that now dominate the economy.

We are in the next transition: the AI era. And the educational system has not caught up.

The Current Crisis

We are moving from an era of search engines to answer engines. This is not a small shift. Search requires the user to evaluate multiple results, to exercise judgment, to develop a relationship with information as something you work toward rather than something you receive. Answer engines present a single response and invite the user to accept it.

The civic implications are enormous. A generation trained to receive answers rather than evaluate them is not a generation prepared for democratic self-governance. Democracy requires the capacity to hold competing claims, to evaluate evidence, to update one's position, to recognize manipulation. These capacities must be taught.

The word "technologist" needs to become common knowledge — part of our education system, part of academic curricula. Not a specialist. A mindset. The understanding that technology is not something that happens to you but something you interact with as a prosumer — both producer and consumer — a citizen who is always asking: how does this work, who built it, what are they optimizing for, and what do I want instead?

People-Positive Directions

1. Embed civic education as a requirement at every grade level — not as a separate subject but as an integrated framework across history, English, science, and social studies.

2. Teach media literacy from elementary school. How to evaluate a source. How to trace a claim. How to recognize manipulation. How to distinguish reporting from opinion, and sponsored content from journalism.

3. Build the concept of "technologist" into standard educational vocabulary and curriculum. Every student should graduate with a basic understanding of how digital systems work, who builds them, and how to interact with them critically.

4. Invest in civically oriented AI education — not just AI coding and engineering but AI citizenship. How do these systems affect democracy? What rights do you have in relation to the data you generate? What does it mean to be a prosumer in a computational economy?

5. Support higher education access for first-generation students, community college students, and adults returning to education mid-career.

6. Create school-based civic imagination programs that ask students to design solutions to real local problems — not hypothetical exercises but actual engagement with city councils, school boards, and civic institutions.

7. Fund the arts as civic infrastructure. The imagination needed to envision a different country is the same imagination that makes literature, music, film, and visual art. Defunding the arts is defunding civic capacity.

8. Restore and protect public libraries as civic infrastructure — not just repositories of books but community learning centers, digital access points, and nonpartisan civic spaces.

On the Search-to-Answer Transition

The specific shift from search engines to answer engines deserves more than a line in a bullet list. It is a civilizational inflection point.

Search required effort. You entered a query. You received results. You evaluated those results. You clicked. You read. You compared. You synthesized. You formed a conclusion. The act of searching was itself an act of developing intellectual agency — the repeated exercise of judgment, of source evaluation, of deciding what counts as evidence and what doesn't.

Answer engines compress that process into a single output. Here is the answer. Accept it. Move on.

This is not neutral. In a democracy, the capacity to hold competing claims simultaneously — to evaluate evidence, to recognize that reasonable people can reach different conclusions from the same facts, to understand that some questions don't have single answers — is not a nice-to-have. It is a load-bearing civic skill.

A generation that has been trained primarily on answer engines is a generation whose capacity for democratic deliberation has been pre-compromised. Not because they are stupid. Not because they are lazy. But because the dominant tool they have used to interact with information has been designed to produce confident, singular answers rather than to model the process of working through complexity.

The prescription — the Habakkuk prescription — is to build the educational infrastructure that trains a different habit. That teaches young people to search, to evaluate, to compare, to doubt productively, to update their positions when evidence requires it. To be prosumers of information rather than passive recipients of answers. To be citizens, not users.

DOMAIN 6: HEALTH, CARE & THE COVENANT OF CARE

The Historical Context

The covenant of care — the idea that a society is responsible for the health and wellbeing of its members — appears in biblical law, in Enlightenment social contract theory, and in the founding documents' language about promoting the general welfare. It is not a radical idea. It is a foundational one.

America has consistently struggled to honor it. We have built a healthcare system that is among the most expensive in the world and among the least equitable. The Affordable Care Act was a partial, imperfect step toward honoring the covenant. Its ongoing survival against political opposition reflects how contested the covenant remains.

The Current Crisis

Healthcare has become a political weapon. The removal of healthcare protections — through executive action, through regulatory rollback, through the appointment of officials who are ideologically opposed to the systems they are charged with administering — is not a policy disagreement. It is a covenant violation.

What we are watching with the HHS leadership is what happens when institutional political ecosystems monetize allegiance. There are investment entities that require and expect a certain level of conformity. What looks like a change of mind is often a change of business relationship. The person who built a public following on vaccine skepticism, who now heads an agency that administers vaccines, has not simply reconsidered. They have been absorbed into a system that has its own requirements.

Dementia is a national health emergency that is not being treated as one. The aging of the population is not a future crisis — it is a current one, with enormous implications for families, for the healthcare system, for the economy, and for the care workers — disproportionately women, disproportionately immigrant, disproportionately low-wage — who are doing the invisible work of keeping people alive and loved.

People-Positive Directions

1. Establish universal baseline healthcare coverage as a constitutional obligation, not a legislative favor.

2. Expand mental health parity so that insurance coverage for mental health services is genuinely equivalent to coverage for physical health services.

3. Treat dementia as a national health emergency. Fund research. Fund caregiver support. Fund community-based care options.

4. Compensate and protect caregivers. Family caregivers provide enormous economic value that is not counted in GDP and not compensated in any systematic way. A care tax credit, Social Security credit for caregiving years, and access to respite services are minimum acknowledgments.

5. Support community health infrastructure — community health workers, neighborhood health clinics, school-based health centers.

6. Reform prescription drug pricing so that Americans do not pay multiples of what citizens in other wealthy countries pay for the same medications.

7. Protect reproductive healthcare as healthcare. The covenant of care does not have exceptions based on gender.

8. Invest in public health preparedness. The pandemic revealed every weakness in the system. The lessons are there. The investment has not followed.

DOMAIN 7: ECONOMIC IDENTITY & THE PROSUMER CLASS

The Historical Context

The post-World War II economic settlement created the conditions in which a middle class could sustain itself. Home ownership. Union wages. Public education. The GI Bill. Social Security. These were not market outcomes. They were policy choices. Investments the country made in its own people, with the understanding that a broad middle class was both an economic asset and a democratic one.

That settlement has been systematically dismantled over the last fifty years. The Powell Memo outlined the strategy. The Reagan era began the implementation. The globalization consensus of the 1990s and 2000s accelerated it. The financialization of the economy — the increasing primacy of financial returns over productive investment — completed the picture.

The Current Crisis

The tech economy created enormous wealth. It did not distribute that wealth broadly. What it distributed was the labor of users — the data, the content, the social graphs, the behavioral patterns — which became the raw material of trillion-dollar valuations. Users generate the value. Platforms capture it.

The prosumer — the person who is simultaneously producer and consumer of digital content — is the economic unit of the current information economy. That person's intellectual contribution, data footprint, and creative output are not legally theirs. They are captured, aggregated, and monetized by platforms that have no legal obligation to share the returns. Every custom GPT thread, every voice memo, every search query, every social media post — these are all raw materials in a production process that the user does not own.

There is a potential class action suit embedded in this reality — a people's dossier of how users actively participated in building these systems, despite all of the terms and conditions, despite all of the fine print, despite the entire legal architecture that was designed to make sure they couldn't claim what was theirs. The fourth branch of government the Constitution doesn't mention — the people's legal standing in relation to the systems that use them — is the next constitutional frontier.

The global tariff crisis of 2025 revealed a related dimension: the elimination of place as an economic factor, the neoliberal consensus that it doesn't matter where goods are made as long as they're made cheaply, has produced dependencies that are now liabilities. The consumer base is deeply dependent on Chinese manufacturing infrastructure — on platforms like Temu and Shein and the supply chains behind them. This is not just an economic vulnerability. It is a strategic one.

A three-pronged trajectory is emerging: physical output and manufacturing; soft power through personal and collective intellectual output that is the American intelligence ecosystem; and hybrid intelligence as economic infrastructure — computational power in genuine partnership with human intelligence, where the human remains the highest-ranking intelligence.

People-Positive Directions

1. Establish a legal framework for user data as intellectual property. The terms and conditions that users routinely sign have never been vetted by a legal representative of the people.

2. Create a User Rights Codex for AI interaction — establishing that the insights, patterns, and outputs produced through human-AI dialogue have co-authorship status and corresponding rights.

3. Invest in domestic manufacturing capacity through strategic industrial policy, not punitive tariffs applied chaotically and without allied consultation.

4. Close the wealth gap through genuine tax reform — closing carried interest loopholes, restoring estate tax rates, creating a wealth tax on assets above a meaningful threshold.

5. Support small business and community economic development as genuine priorities. Small businesses employ more Americans than Fortune 500 companies.

6. Create a Universal Basic Services floor — universal access to the services that make economic participation possible: broadband, transportation, housing, healthcare, education.

7. Recognize and compensate the intelligence contributor economy — the people whose knowledge, creativity, and data are powering AI systems — through a regulatory framework that requires platforms to disclose and share the economic value generated by user contributions.

8. Treat economic resilience as a national security issue. Supply chain vulnerability, manufacturing dependency, and strategic technology gaps are not just economic problems. They are democratic ones.

On Computational Authorship and the Intellectual Contribution Economy

There is a specific conversation that has been happening for years — in legal circles, in academic circles, in the privacy of anyone who has ever contributed substantially to an AI system's training data and then watched the platform profit from that contribution without any recognition or compensation.

The question of computational authorship is the question of who owns the intelligence that emerges from human-AI collaboration. When a person spends years developing a distinctive way of thinking and communicating — a specific synthesis of information, a particular mode of insight, a recognizable intellectual voice — and that thinking is captured in millions of words of interaction with AI systems, who owns the resulting intelligence?

The platforms say: we do. The terms of service say: we do. The legal framework, such as it is, says: it's complicated, but effectively, we do.

This is not acceptable. Not morally. Not democratically. Not in a country that was founded on the principle that a person's labor — intellectual and physical — is their own.

The custom GPT threads, the voice memos, the accumulated ChatGPT conversations that constitute a person's intellectual output over years — these are not merely inputs to a system. They are a corpus. They are a record of intellectual labor. They are, in the most meaningful sense of the word, a person's mind as it has developed and been documented in real time.

The argument that a custom LLM configuration that explicitly recognizes a user as a "commendable" synthesizer of complex information — that this recognition is just a marketing output of the platform rather than an acknowledgment of the user's actual intellectual contribution — is the same argument that said the cotton picker's labor was just a byproduct of the owner's investment in the field.

The intelligence contributor economy is real. The legal framework to recognize and compensate it does not yet exist. Power 2026 says: build it.

DOMAIN 8: HOUSING & BELONGING

The Historical Context

The Fair Housing Act of 1968 was passed in the week following the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. — one of the clearest examples in American history of grief converted into legislation. It was also imperfectly enforced, systematically circumvented through redlining, exclusionary zoning, predatory lending, and the racial wealth gap in home ownership that persists to this day.

Home ownership is the primary vehicle through which American families build intergenerational wealth. The policies that structured housing markets in the twentieth century — the GI Bill, the FHA loan guarantees, redlining, the interstate highway system — were not race-neutral. They built the wealth of some families and denied it to others. That inheritance has not been remedied. It has been compounded.

The Current Crisis

Housing costs have reached a generational crisis point. The financialization of housing — the treatment of homes primarily as investment vehicles rather than as places to live — has priced millions of Americans out of the ownership market and put enormous pressure on the rental market. When hedge funds acquire single-family homes at scale and rent them back to families who cannot afford to buy them, the housing market is no longer functioning as a housing market. It is functioning as an extraction machine.

Belonging is what housing provides that rent numbers do not capture. Stability. Community. School continuity. The ability to know your neighbors. The ability to feel at home in the place where you live. These are not luxuries. They are the conditions of a functioning civic life, of the We the Neighbors infrastructure that democracy requires.

People-Positive Directions

1. Zone for housing — local zoning reform to allow more housing, at more price points, in more neighborhoods.

2. Limit institutional ownership of single-family homes.

3. Invest in public and community land trust housing — permanently affordable models that remove units from the speculative market.

4. Expand and protect tenant rights — just cause eviction protections, habitability standards, anti-retaliation provisions.

5. Create a first-generation homebuyer program that specifically addresses the wealth gap.

6. Fund community-based anti-displacement strategies. The right of existing communities to stay in the places they have built should be weighed in every development decision.

7. Regulate short-term rental platforms in ways that protect the long-term rental market in high-demand communities.

DOMAIN 9: CIVIC INFRASTRUCTURE & WE THE NEIGHBORS

The Historical Context

The original civic infrastructure of America was not government. It was community. Churches. Mutual aid societies. Volunteer fire companies. The Grange. The labor hall. The NAACP chapter. The neighborhood. These were the institutions through which ordinary Americans exercised civic power before they had formal political power — and often in the spaces from which formal political power had excluded them.

Organizing 101 — and this is the foundational truth beneath every successful civic movement — begins with: introduce yourself to your neighbor. Before the petition. Before the march. Before the campaign. Before the vote. You begin by showing up in proximity to another human being and saying: I am here. I am your neighbor. What do you need? What do I need? What do we share?

The Current Crisis

We the Neighbors is the only politics that ultimately works at scale. Not We the Party Members. Not We the Donors. Not We the Highly Engaged Activists. We the Neighbors — the people who live next to each other, who share the same school district, who use the same roads, who drink the same water, who are affected by the same zoning decisions — are the basic unit of democratic life.

Social trust has declined. Civic participation in organized community life has declined. The number of Americans who say they know their neighbors has declined. The connective tissue of civic life is eroding. And the Influence Matrix profits from its erosion — because people who don't know their neighbors are easy to frighten about them.

The polarization we are experiencing is partly manufactured by media and political ecosystems that profit from division. But it is also sustained by genuine disconnection. The antidote to the politics of fear is the practice of proximity. You cannot hate your neighbor if you know your neighbor. You cannot demonize your community if you are present in it. You cannot be a passive recipient of political narratives about who your fellow Americans are if you know your fellow Americans personally.

People-Positive Directions

1. Invest in third places — libraries, community centers, parks, public gathering spaces that belong to everyone.

2. Support neighborhood-level civic infrastructure — block associations, neighborhood councils, community land trusts — with modest public investment and genuine institutional respect.

3. Create civic service pathways for young people that are not military and not performative — genuine community service that builds skills, relationships, and civic identity.

4. Reform the design of cities and neighborhoods to support human-scale interaction. Urban design matters to democracy.

5. Protect and expand the commons — clean water, clean air, public parks, public libraries, public schools, public transportation.

6. Create mechanisms for deliberative democracy at the local level — structured processes through which neighbors can work through disagreements and build the trust that makes governance possible.

7. Recognize and support the role of faith communities in civic life. Faith communities are the original We the Neighbors infrastructure, doing work that no other institution is doing — feeding people, caring for the elderly, educating children, holding communities together through crisis.

8. Honor the intergenerational covenant — the understanding that decisions made today will be inherited by people who are not yet born, and that this inheritance carries a moral obligation.

DOMAIN 10: THE NEXT 250 — CONSTITUTIONAL IMAGINATION

The Historical Context

The Constitution has been amended 27 times. It was designed to change. The amendment process was not an emergency exit — it was a planned door. The founders knew they hadn't gotten it right. They knew the document was a beginning. They built in the mechanism for it to evolve.

The 10th Amendment's reservation of power — "or to the people" — is the seed of a civic constitutional imagination that we have never fully activated. What does it mean to exercise the powers reserved to the people? What would it look like to convene a constitutional convention from the people's side — not from the parties, not from the state legislatures, but from a broad, representative cross-section of American citizens who are charged with examining what the next 250 years of self-governance should look like?

There are rumblings about this already. The ERA. Citizens United. Electoral College reform. The appointment process for Supreme Court justices. These are all constitutional-level questions that are beginning to generate constitutional-level thinking.

The Current Crisis

The next president of the United States will be the 50th. Fifty presidents in 250 years — an average of one every five years. And thinking about goals for the country that begin with that 50th presidency, that align with the 250th anniversary of the founding, that set up the architecture for the next 250 years — this is not premature. It is exactly the right moment to think this way.

There is a lawfare problem. Legal mechanisms designed to serve justice are being used as weapons of personal vendetta. Executive orders are being used to circumvent congressional authority. The pro bono legal services extracted from major law firms under threat of financial retaliation are payments in kind — services that will be used for the political benefit of the administration rather than the public interest. The revolving door between the administration and the legal system is spinning faster than the constitutional architects anticipated.

And yet: every one of these abuses is visible. They are happening in the light. The constitutional structure, strained as it is, is producing the friction it was designed to produce. The courts are pushing back. The states are pushing back. The people are pushing back. The system is working at its margins, which means the margins need to be strengthened.

People-Positive Directions

1. Begin a national conversation about constitutional revision — not panic-driven but thoughtful, long-range engagement with what the document needs for the next 250 years.

2. Elevate the 10th Amendment's "or to the people" language as a civic organizing principle. Power not given to the federal government and not assigned to the states belongs to the people.

3. Establish constitutional literacy as a civic goal. Every American should be able to read and understand the Constitution as a citizen.

4. Create a Citizens' Constitutional Commission — a nonpartisan, representative body charged with producing a public assessment of where the Constitution is working, where it is not, and what revisions the body politic should consider.

5. Explore personhood protections in the age of artificial intelligence — what rights individuals have in relation to systems that use their data, their intellectual output, and their behavioral patterns.

6. Protect the independent judiciary through term limits for Supreme Court justices, mandatory recusal standards, transparency in internal decision-making, and democratic checks on appointment power.

7. Commit to the long view. The Constitution was written by people who were thinking in long time. We need to think in long time too. The decisions made in 2026 will be inherited by people not yet born. That is both the weight and the privilege of citizenship in a democracy.

8. Move toward a Declaration of Freedom — a new moral founding document for the 250th year that names what we know now, commits to what we value, and charges the next generation with carrying it forward.

INTERLUDE: THE SPIRITUAL FOUNDATION

Before the call to action. Before the list. Before the ten domains become a to-do list and the manifesto becomes a checklist — there is this.

Willingness.

The verse from Philippians: "For it is God who works in you to will and to act in order to fulfill his good purpose." What was emphasized was the willingness part. The idea that the desire itself — the will to obey, to follow, to seek, to continue, to sustain, to dwell, to walk, to remain, to be, to share, to speak, to love, to talk, to write, to publish, to promote, to sit, to wait, to go, to stop — is stirred up in us. It is not manufactured. It is not performed. It is received.

This matters for a civic document. Because civic engagement is often talked about as obligation — duty, responsibility, the thing you're supposed to do because citizenship requires it. And yes. But obligation alone does not sustain movements. Obligation alone does not build the long view. Obligation alone did not keep people on the Edmund Pettus Bridge long enough for the cameras to arrive and the world to see.

What sustains movements is something that feels, to the people doing the sustaining, less like duty and more like calling. Less like civic checklist and more like divine assignment. The willingness that is stirred up in you — that is inconvenient, uncomfortable, poorly timed, requiring you to show up when showing up is the last thing you feel prepared to do — is the willingness that builds something.

The book "Hope Maketh Not Ashamed" has been in progress for years — written in the Reminders app, in voice memos, in ChatGPT threads, in the margins of a life that got very busy with the work of surviving. It is a book about delusional ambition in middle age. About the hopes we leave on the side of the road like litter. About retracing our steps to pick them up, to curate them, to write them plainly into a compendium, to shake the dust out of them, to rearrange them, to put them in a narrative order that makes sense of the years gone by and the years still ahead.

The book is also about succession planning — which is nothing but a fight between fear and freedom. Fear masquerades in different ways in the succession planning conversation. But at its core, the question is always: do I trust what comes after me? Do I plant seeds I won't live to see grow? Do I do the work of the generation knowing that the generation I'm doing it for is not yet born?

Moses did not see the Promised Land. He did the work of leading a people for forty years in the wilderness, and then he handed the charge to Joshua, and he died. Not in defeat. In completion. He did what was his to do.

We are all Moses and we are all Joshua. We are inheriting the work of the generation that came before us and we are charging the generation that comes after. The charge is both temporal and eternal: this is what we know. This is what we have learned. This is what we are passing on. The rest is yours.

The spiritual foundation of Power 2026 is not that God is on our side politically. It is that human dignity is a sacred value — that the founding documents, imperfect as they are, were reaching for something sacred when they said all men are created equal and endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable rights. The reaching matters. The imperfection of the reaching is the story. The continued effort to close the gap between what we declared and what we have delivered is the work.

That work is not partisan. It is prophetic. And the prophetic tradition — from Habakkuk to King — has always said the same thing: write it down. Make it plain. So that those who come after you can run with it, even when you no longer can.

PART FOUR: THE CALL

What Any Individual Can Do

This is where some documents stop. They lay out the vision, they name the domains, they prescribe the directions — and then they say: now vote.

That is not enough.

Voting is the floor of participation, not the ceiling. It is the minimum, not the maximum. It is necessary. It is not sufficient. And in a political system designed to make your vote matter as little as possible through gerrymandering, closed primaries, and suppression — your vote is most powerful when it is accompanied by the full weight of your citizenship.

Here is what any individual can do, starting now, regardless of party registration, regardless of income, regardless of how tired and discouraged the last several years have made you:

Read the founding documents. The Declaration of Independence. The Constitution. The Bill of Rights. Not in a class. On your phone. On your lunch break. They are short. They are yours. You should know what they say. The text is public domain. It belongs to you. Read it. Know your representatives. Not just your senator and your congressperson. Your state representative. Your city council member. Your school board member. These are the people who make the decisions that affect your daily life most directly. Know their names. Know their records. Know their donors. Know the difference between what they say and what they vote. Introduce yourself to your neighbor. This is not a metaphor. Actually do it. Knock on the door. Learn the name. This is the foundational act of civic life. Every movement that has ever worked started here. We the Neighbors is not a slogan. It is an instruction. Register to vote. If you are not registered, register. If you are registered, check that your registration is current. Then understand that voting is the minimum. Vote in the primaries. The primary is where the real decisions are made. In most states, the general election is already decided by the primary outcome. If your state has open primaries, use them. If your state has closed primaries and you are registered independent, know your options. The primary is where power is first seized. Show up at town halls. Ask questions on the record. Make your elected officials articulate in public where the money comes from and where it goes. Create something. Write. Speak. Organize. Build. The intelligence contributor economy is not just a policy domain — it is an invitation. Your knowledge, your experience, your voice are resources the country needs. Use them. Talk to people you disagree with. Not to convert them. To understand them. The greatest civic danger of our current moment is not disagreement. It is the replacement of disagreement with contempt — with the total inability to recognize that the person on the other side of the political divide is also a citizen, also affected, also trying to make sense of a country that is genuinely hard to make sense of. Refuse to be entertainment. The Influence Matrix profits from your outrage. Your despair is a revenue stream. Your engagement — even your negative engagement — funds the system you're mad at. Choose your engagement carefully. Put your energy where it builds something.

No One Gets Off Scot Free

Let this be the refrain of the 250th year.

Not the Republicans who have embraced a vision of concentrated executive power at the expense of every other branch of government. Not the Republicans who voted against certifying a free and fair election. Not the Republicans who have stood silent while the legal and regulatory framework of American democratic life has been systematically dismantled.

Not the Democrats who presided over the conditions that made the backlash possible and then seemed surprised when it arrived. Not the Democrats who ran a candidate who had visibly declined without permitting a genuine democratic primary to produce an alternative. Not the Democrats who still speak primarily to their base and call it national leadership.

Not the media, which has reduced the most consequential civic story of our lifetime to a personality drama, a ratings play, and a never-ending cycle of outrage content. Not the media, which calls itself "the press" while operating primarily as an engagement maximizer. Not the media, which employs talented journalists and then requires them to operate within commercial incentives that systematically distort the story.

Not the platforms — not Twitter/X, not Facebook/Meta, not Google, not TikTok — which have profited enormously from the division they amplify and the civic damage they accelerate, and which have systematically resisted accountability under the shield of Section 230.

Not the lobbyists and the dark money networks and the think tanks that front for corporate and ideological interests while presenting themselves as civic institutions. Not the Powell Memo's children, who have spent fifty years executing a strategy for which they should at least be honest about the authorship.

Not the independent voters who chose to opt out without choosing to build something. Not the people who know better and stay quiet. Not the people who have decided that cynicism is more sophisticated than civic commitment.

Not us.

No one gets off scot free. This is a collective failure. It requires a collective remedy.

The Moral Contest

Before answering why 2026, it's worth naming what kind of contest we are in.

Presidential elections are moral contests. Period. Not primarily policy contests. Not primarily competence contests. Moral contests. The reason Barack Obama won — twice — was not that he had the superior healthcare plan or the more sophisticated foreign policy doctrine. It was that he represented a moral possibility. A moral claim about what America is and could be. The reason his election was transformative was not governmental. It was moral. He was proof that the founding aspiration — all men are created equal — was still being tested, still being expanded, still alive.

The reason the 2020 election was a moral contest was that the alternative to Biden was the continuation of a moral posture — toward democracy, toward truth, toward the peaceful transfer of power — that a significant portion of the country found disqualifying. And the reason 2024 was so devastating was that when the moral case should have been easiest to make, the institutional Democratic Party made it difficult by failing to run an actual primary and denying the electorate the moral clarity of a genuine choice.

The 2026 midterm is also a moral contest. Not primarily a referendum on the Trump administration's policies, though it is that too. It is a moral referendum on whether the people of this country — not the party, not the donor class, not the professional political class — believe that the experiment of self-governance is worth fighting for. That the Constitution is worth defending. That the covenant of care is worth honoring. That we the neighbors are worth showing up for.

The moral case for 2026 does not belong to either party. It belongs to every American who believes that the next 250 years should be better than the last 250 — more just, more true to the founding promise, more inclusive of the people who have always been here but haven't always been counted.

That is the moral contest of 2026. And we are all in it. No one gets off scot free.

Why 2026 Is the Appointed Time

"For the revelation awaits an appointed time; it speaks of the end and will not prove false. Though it linger, wait for it; it will certainly come and will not delay."

We are at the halfway point of the first five hundred years of American self-governance. The midterm of 2026 will determine who controls Congress for the next two years — during which the most consequential decisions about the direction of American democracy will be made. The America 250 anniversary will either pass as a commercial opportunity or emerge as a genuine civic rededication. The 50th president is two elections away. The constitutional convention rumblings are growing.

The window is now. The appointed time is now. The vision needs to be written down before the moment passes, before the herald needs to run with it and finds nothing to run with.

The stakes are specific. We are not speaking abstractly about democracy or the republic or the American experiment. We are speaking concretely about the 2026 midterm elections, about the 400-plus congressional seats that will be contested, about the state legislatures that will redraw maps, about the ballot initiatives that will determine how states govern themselves, about the school board races that will determine what children are taught about this country and its history.

2026 is the appointed time because the next 250 years begin to take shape in the decisions made between now and then. The seeds planted in 2026 will be the trees under which our children's children will take shelter — or not. The foundation laid in 2026 will be the one on which the 50th president builds — or doesn't.

Write the vision. Make it plain. So that a herald may run with it.

The Reckoning We Owe

There is a cultural reckoning that Power 2026 does not avoid.

We are a country that has been largely unwilling to sit with the discomfort of its own history. To stay with the joke long enough to understand what it reveals. To confront the inheritance — not with guilt as a permanent posture, but with clarity as a prerequisite for action.

The Golden Girls — one of the rare sitcoms that actually engaged with race in all its complexity and assumption — had Blanche deliver a joke: "There are two things you never sell to a friend — a car and a slave. 'Cause if either one of them quits working, you'll never hear the end of it." And then the kicker: "'Course, they hanged my great granddaddy. He said a lot of things he shouldn't have."

That is not a joke to skip past. That is Blanche acknowledging — however obliquely, however twisted through the mechanisms of humor — that she comes from a Southern family steeped in racism, and that history has consequences. The writers of The Golden Girls were doing something that modern commentators — even when reviewing The Golden Girls — are not willing to do: staying with the discomfort long enough to let it say something.

We need to stay with the discomfort.

Not as self-flagellation. Not as paralysis. Not as the kind of performative contrition that serves the person confessing more than the people who were harmed. But as honest accounting. The reckoning we owe is the reckoning of seeing clearly — the two microfiches at once, the power map that shows how the movement leaders ended up outnumbered at the implementation table, the way the moral clarity of the Civil Rights Movement got translated into technocratic language and then, fifty years later, rolled back as though it were just a regulatory regime rather than a covenant.

The reckoning we owe is the same reckoning that Emerson was calling for in Self-Reliance — not the rugged individualism that has been made of it, but the actual Emersonian claim: that society has an aversion to self-reliance and requires conformity, not liberty and freedom. That to be true to yourself — to your actual assessment of what is happening, to your actual values, to your actual vision — is always a counter-cultural act.

America's founders were, at the moment of the founding, the people. They were not the elite figures we have made them in retrospect. They were workers and tillers and men of varying degrees of property and principle. They were the people. And they were trying to build something for the people — which means us. All of us. Including the people they hadn't yet imagined as people.

The reckoning we owe is to see them as they were — human, flawed, reaching — and to extend that same grace to ourselves. We are also human. We are also flawed. We are also reaching. And we are also, all of us, responsible for what gets built from here.

The Closing Charge

We are the generation that remembers before and now dares to design the after.

We are the ones who watched the towers fall in real time, and young enough to remember the dial-up tones of our first online dreams. We grew up with pledge-of-allegiance mornings and climate anxiety afternoons. We had civics class and Columbine. We learned voting rights and voter suppression in the same decade. We are old enough to have seen what this country was and young enough to be responsible for what it becomes.

We are the bookend generation. We inherited the analog and now architect in the algorithmic. We remember what acid rain felt like and what chatbots say. And we are the ones caught in the middle — of grief and possibility, of debt and design.

We are not the lost generation. We are the locating generation. We know where we are on the map. We know what time it is.

We built this country. All of us. With labor that was often enslaved, often undercompensated, often invisible. With genius that was often ignored, often appropriated, often denied. With sacrifice that was often unacknowledged, often unrewarded, often met with violence.

We built this. And we are building it still.

We are not the people who inherited a finished project and are charged with maintaining it. We are the people in the middle of a project that has never been finished, has never been fully true to its own promises, and has always — always — required the next generation to demand more of it than the previous generation left behind.

The founders were, after all, THE PEOPLE. Little did they know that in creating rules for their own enrichment, they inadvertently enriched the lives of generations far beyond the scope of their imagination.

We are among those generations. And we are also the next founders — the people who will determine what the American Project means for the second 250 years, what values it honors, what covenants it keeps, what it chooses to finally fulfill.

Both parties are implicated. Both parties are necessary. We are not replacing the parties. We are above them, in the constitutional sense — because We the Neighbors, We the People, are the source of the power that the parties are merely authorized to wield in our name.

And we are withdrawing the authorization for business as usual.

We are issuing a new authorization: for the American Project.

Not the Republican project. Not the Democratic project. Not the left's project. Not the right's project. Not the Heritage Foundation's project. Not the progressive coalition's project. Not Team System.

Team Human.

We are 250 years in. The next 250 are ours to write.

Independence from the lie that we have no say.

Interdependence as strategy, not sentiment.

Inheritance as something we write — not just receive.

We have been preparing for this moment for longer than we knew. Every conversation we had about why the party was failing. Every vote we cast in the midterms when the presidential election was still two years away. Every time we read the Constitution and noticed something we hadn't noticed before. Every time we got off the phone with our congressperson's office and felt like maybe something moved. Every time we looked at our neighbor and thought: we are in this together.

We are in this together.

The spiritual warfare is real. The forces of fear and freedom are colliding. The soul of the nation is in play. But this is not a moment for despair. This is a moment for the discipline of hope — not the hope that waits passively for things to get better, but the hope that writes it down, makes it plain, and sends a herald running.

The revolution we are inviting is not a revolution of fire and flags. No one will fall here. This is not a eulogy. This is a birth. A reawakening. A mapping of ideas as worlds — not as battlefields, but as breathing systems. We enter with not weapons, but questions. With belief in life itself. And with the conviction that every system we've built can be unbuilt and rebuilt — without harm, without erasure, without loss of love or legacy.

We are Team Human. Not Team System. Not Team Party. Not Team Platform.

Team Human means: we choose the human over the algorithm. The neighbor over the narrative. The long view over the next news cycle. The covenant over the contract. The people over the parties.

And we choose it together.

So go find your stool. Sit with your purpose. Then stand up and speak from it like it's yours.

Because it is.
